Era 1 · 1960s to 1979

The Mainframe AgeOne computer. Many people. Zero choice.

Computing existed — but ordinary people had no direct access to it. Everything ran on massive, room-sized machines. Your "terminal" was just a keyboard and a screen. The brain lived somewhere else entirely. IT was a priesthood. Users were supplicants.

The Model

Centralised everything

Compute, storage, and logic all lived in one place — the mainframe. "Dumb terminals" connected users to that central brain. No local processing. No local storage. You submitted a job; you waited; you collected your printout.

IBM's System/360 (1964) became the defining platform — the same operating system across different hardware sizes. This was the AWS of its day: scalable, shared, and controlled.

Why This Matters Today

The model never died — it just moved

Azure Virtual Desktop, Windows 365, Citrix DaaS — all are philosophically identical to the mainframe model. Powerful compute in a datacentre. A thin device at the edge. Session-based access to a shared environment.

Every time someone says "cloud desktops are the future," they're rediscovering 1965. The genius was always the architecture — we just needed the internet to make it work globally.

Who Had Access

Governments, banks, universities

You needed millions of dollars and a dedicated machine room. NASA used IBM mainframes for Apollo. Banks ran overnight batch processing. Access was a privilege.

The Management Reality

Centralised = controllable

Ironically, this era had the best security posture of any period in EUC history. Everything was in one place, owned by IT, with zero end-user autonomy. The problems came later — when we gave people power.

Era 2 · 1980 to 1989

The PC RevolutionPower to the people. Chaos to IT.

The personal computer put computing directly in people's hands for the first time. It was a decade of extraordinary creativity, explosive growth, and the birth of nearly every problem IT still deals with today. The pendulum swung completely from central control to individual freedom.

The Catalyst

IBM PC lands on the desk — 1981

IBM launched the IBM Personal Computer in August 1981. Critically, they used an open architecture and outsourced the OS to a small company called Microsoft. This decision — made for cost reasons — accidentally created the entire PC industry as we know it.

The Operating System War

Microsoft wins everything

MS-DOS shipped on every IBM PC clone. By the mid-80s, there were hundreds of PC manufacturers, all running Microsoft's OS. Apple chose the opposite path — beautiful, closed, premium. Microsoft chose open, ubiquitous, and dominant. That choice shaped EUC for 40 years.

The IT Crisis

Shadow IT is born

With no central management, people installed anything. Floppy disks spread viruses. Data lived on local drives and vanished when they died. Nobody had backups. Nobody had a software inventory. IT went from running one computer to trying to manage thousands — with no tools to do it.

🧬 The DNA Moment — The Freedom vs Control Tension

The PC revolution created the central paradox that defines all of EUC history: the more freedom users have, the harder IT's job becomes. Every subsequent era is an attempt to rebalance this equation — giving users enough productivity and autonomy while keeping IT able to secure, manage, and support what they've deployed. This tension never fully resolves. It just shifts.

Era 3 · 1990 to 1999

Networks, Citrix & the InternetConnecting the PCs. Centralising the apps. The web changes everything.

The 1990s wired the world together. Local networks connected desks. Servers returned to the picture. Citrix invented a way to deliver Windows over any connection. And then the internet arrived and rewrote every assumption about where software could live and how it would be delivered.

🏢
1990–1994 · The LAN Era
Connecting the desks
Local Area Networks (LANs) connected PCs within buildings. Novell NetWare dominated file and print sharing. The "client-server" model emerged: your PC ran applications, a central server handled data and shared services. IT regained some control — but still had to physically visit every machine to make any change.
🔶
1989 · The Citrix Founding Moment
Ed Iacobucci founds Citrix Systems
Citrix was founded in Richardson, Texas in April 1989 by Ed Iacobucci, a former IBM engineer who had led the OS/2 operating system project. With $3M in venture funding, his vision was multi-user Windows computing delivered from a central server. The first product shipped in 1991. By 1993, Citrix acquired a Novell remote-access product and evolved it into WinView — the first commercially successful thin-client Windows delivery. WinFrame followed in 1995. The core insight: why deploy and manage software on 1,000 PCs when you can run it once on a server and let 1,000 people connect? Citrix would dominate enterprise app delivery for the next 25+ years.
🌍
1993–1995 · The Internet Arrives
The World Wide Web changes everything
Mosaic (1993) and Netscape (1994) turned the internet into something civilians could use. Email became mainstream. The browser became a new kind of thin client — a universal application runner that required no local installation. The seeds of SaaS were planted. The question of "where does software live?" became genuinely complicated.
🪟
1997–1999 · Microsoft Terminal Services
Microsoft builds Citrix into Windows
Microsoft licensed Citrix technology and built multi-user session capabilities into Windows NT 4.0 Terminal Server Edition. Citrix responded brilliantly: rather than fighting, they became the value-add layer on top of Microsoft, adding ICA protocol (far superior to RDP), load balancing, application publishing, and enterprise management. This partnership-competition model defined the next 20 years.
🔧
Late 1990s · Endpoint Management Emerges
The first attempts to manage PCs at scale
As organisations grew to thousands of PCs, IT faced the impossible task of keeping them all patched and configured. Microsoft SMS (Systems Management Server), LANDesk, and IBM Tivoli emerged — the ancestors of modern Microsoft Intune and endpoint management platforms. Scripting and automation began to replace physical visits, but tools were primitive.

Era 4 · 2000 to 2012

The VDI & Virtualisation EraThe golden age of the datacentre. The curse of complexity.

VMware transformed how servers worked. Citrix matured into an enterprise empire. VDI arrived — the dream of giving every user their own virtual PC in a datacentre. And alongside it, the first serious attempts at endpoint management at scale. The vision was elegant. The reality was brutal.

The VMware Revolution

One server. Many virtual machines.

VMware (founded 1998, mainstream by 2003–2005) made server virtualisation accessible. One physical server could run dozens of virtual machines simultaneously. This changed datacentre economics forever and proved that abstraction — the OS doesn't need to know what hardware it runs on — was a viable architecture.

VMware's ESX/ESXi hypervisor became the datacentre standard. Organisations virtualised their servers first — then asked: why not do the same with desktops?

The VDI Promise

One image. Infinite desktops.

Virtual Desktop Infrastructure promised to centralise the PC itself. Manage one golden image, deliver it to any device on earth. Security becomes centralised. Disaster recovery becomes trivial. Any device, anywhere, gets the same consistent Windows experience.

VMware View (later Horizon) and Citrix XenDesktop went head-to-head. Both required enormous on-premises investment in compute, storage, networking, and specialist skills.

"VDI is the future of desktop computing — and it always will be." — A joke that circulated for a decade, reflecting how often VDI projects missed their goals
Why VDI Was So Hard

The seven deadly sins

  • Massive upfront infrastructure cost ($500K–$5M+)
  • Complex capacity planning — boot storms, login storms
  • Storage I/O bottlenecks during peak demand
  • Application compatibility and sequencing nightmares
  • Profiles: where does the user's data and settings live?
  • 6–8 specialist skill sets required to deploy and run
  • User perception: "It feels slower than my laptop"

The Optimisation Ecosystem

A cottage industry of fixes

VDI complexity created an entire ecosystem of companies solving problems the platforms couldn't: profile management (FSLogix, Liquidware), performance monitoring (ControlUp, Lakeside), application delivery (App-V, ThinApp), OS optimisation tools. Each added cost and complexity. The stack grew.

📦 New Concept: Application Virtualisation & Layering

One of VDI's biggest headaches was application management. Installing software into a golden image meant rebuilding and redeploying it constantly. Application virtualisation (Microsoft App-V, Citrix Streaming, VMware ThinApp) let you package apps into containers that could be "streamed" to virtual desktops without traditional installation — separating the app from the OS image.

Application layering (Citrix App Layering, Liquidware FlexApp, VMware App Volumes) took this further — letting admins compose a desktop from modular layers: a base OS layer, an application layer, and a user personalisation layer. This reduced image management from dozens of images to a handful of composable layers. The concept lives on in modern AVD image management today.

The Profile Problem — A Persistent Pain

FSLogix: the saviour of VDI profiles

User profiles in VDI were notoriously problematic. They were large, slow to load, and would corrupt. FSLogix (founded 2012, acquired by Microsoft November 2018) solved this with profile containers — VHD-mounted profile disks that loaded instantly and didn't corrupt. Microsoft made it free with every Microsoft 365 subscription. It now ships with every M365 licence and is a core component of every AVD deployment.

Era 5 · 2013 to 2019

The Cloud Transition & Convergence EraSaaS, mobile, SD-WAN, Zero Trust — the stack gets rebuilt from scratch.

This was the decade that broke nearly every assumption EUC had operated on. Cloud computing matured. Applications moved to SaaS. Smartphones challenged the PC. The perimeter network — the thing that made traditional EUC security possible — dissolved. And Microsoft reinvented itself under Satya Nadella in the most consequential pivot in tech history.

Microsoft's Critical Pivot — 2014

Satya Nadella changes everything

When Nadella became CEO in February 2014, he declared Microsoft a "mobile-first, cloud-first" company. This wasn't marketing — it was structural. Office moved from perpetual licence to Office 365 subscription SaaS. Azure became the company's centre of gravity. Active Directory began its migration to the cloud as Azure Active Directory (Entra ID).

Most critically for EUC: Microsoft began investing in delivering Windows itself from Azure — setting the stage for everything that comes next.

SaaS Displaces Everything

If apps are in the cloud, why isn't the desktop?

Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow, Slack, Zoom — application after application moved from on-premises software to browser-based SaaS. This fundamentally changed the VDI conversation. The primary justification for VDI had been centralising application management — but if applications were already in the cloud, what was VDI actually doing?

The rationale for cloud desktops strengthened: if compute, storage, and applications are all in Azure, why is your Windows desktop still running on a server in your building?

🌐 New Concept: SD-WAN — Why Networking Became an EUC Problem

Traditional enterprise networking assumed users were in offices, connected to corporate datacentres via MPLS leased lines. Traffic flow: endpoint → office LAN → MPLS → datacentre → internet (via a central security stack). This worked in a world of on-premises VDI and on-premises applications.

Then SaaS arrived. Suddenly, the most-used applications (Office 365, Salesforce, Zoom) lived on the internet — not in the datacentre. Backhauling all that traffic through a datacentre firewall was expensive and created terrible user experience. SD-WAN (Software-Defined Wide Area Network) decoupled the network from physical hardware, allowed intelligent traffic routing (send Office 365 direct to the internet, send sensitive traffic through the secure hub), and created the concept of "local internet breakout." EUC professionals had to understand networking because networking now directly determined whether their cloud desktops performed well.

Mobile & BYOD

The device you don't own

iPhones and iPads entered the enterprise. Organisations had to manage devices they hadn't bought and couldn't control at an OS level. Mobile Device Management (MDM) became a new category. DEX Emerges

The first wave of Digital Employee Experience

As the workspace became more complex (VDI + SaaS + mobile + physical PCs), organisations lost visibility into whether employees could actually do their jobs. Lakeside Software (SysTrack) pioneered Digital Employee Experience (DEX) monitoring — measuring what users actually experienced, not just what IT thought was happening. This was DEX's quiet genesis.

🔒 New Concept: Zero Trust — The Death of the Perimeter

Traditional security was built on a perimeter model: everything inside the corporate network is trusted; everything outside is untrusted. VPNs extended that trusted perimeter to remote workers. Firewalls kept the bad actors out.

This model collapsed in the 2010s. Users worked everywhere. Applications lived on the internet. The perimeter was dissolving. John Kindervag at Forrester articulated the response in 2010: Zero Trust — "Never trust, always verify." Instead of trusting a network location, you trust an identity. Every access request — regardless of where it comes from — must be verified through identity, device health, context, and risk signals. For EUC, this meant every time a user tried to access an application, the system checked: who is this user? What device are they on? Is it compliant? Where are they connecting from? What are they trying to access?

Zero Trust isn't a product — it's a philosophy. But Microsoft's implementation of it — through Entra ID (Azure AD), Conditional Access, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, and Intune compliance policies — became the practical EUC expression of Zero Trust at scale.

Conditional Access — EUC's Zero Trust Engine

The policy layer that connects everything

Conditional Access (built into Microsoft Entra ID) is the practical implementation of Zero Trust in most enterprise environments. It evaluates: user identity + device compliance state + location + application being accessed + risk score — and then decides: allow, block, or require additional verification (MFA, device enrolment).

For EUC professionals, understanding Conditional Access is now non-negotiable. It determines what users can access, from where, on which devices. AVD and Windows 365 environments are typically enforced via Conditional Access policies — making it a core part of every cloud desktop deployment.

🏗️ New Concept: SASE — Security & Network Converge

By the late 2010s, organisations had assembled a patchwork of network and security tools: SD-WAN for traffic routing, CASB (Cloud Access Security Broker) for SaaS visibility, Secure Web Gateway for web filtering, ZTNA (Zero Trust Network Access) for application access. Gartner's Neil MacDonald and Joe Skorupa coined SASE (Secure Access Service Edge) in 2019 — a framework for converging these capabilities into a single cloud-delivered service.

For EUC, SASE matters because it defines how users securely access applications — whether those applications are in Azure (where AVD runs), in SaaS (Office 365, Salesforce), or in on-premises datacentres. SASE vendors (Zscaler, Palo Alto Prisma Access, Cloudflare) often sit between the user's AVD session and the applications they're trying to reach, doing security inspection without hairpinning through a datacentre.

📊 DEX Comes of Age — Digital Employee Experience Becomes Strategic

Digital Employee Experience (DEX) measures how well technology actually serves employees — not just whether systems are "up," but whether users can do their work productively, with acceptable performance, minimal friction, and a good experience. It's the difference between "the server is running" and "the user can open an application in under 3 seconds."

DEX platforms (Lakeside SysTrack, ControlUp, Nexthink, Aternity) instrument endpoints and virtual desktops to capture thousands of performance signals per session — login duration, app launch times, session reliability, network quality, resource consumption. This data feeds both reactive IT support (why is this user having a bad day?) and proactive optimisation (what patterns predict problems before users complain?).

Post-COVID, DEX became a C-suite conversation: with remote and hybrid work, executives realised they could no longer walk the floor to assess productivity. DEX data became the evidence base for IT investment decisions. Gartner and IDC both created formal DEX market categories. Vendors like Nexthink raised hundreds of millions. And Microsoft's own tooling (Endpoint Analytics, now part of Intune) embedded basic DEX capabilities natively.

Infrastructure as Code

Clicking through consoles is dead

Modern cloud infrastructure — including AVD environments — is increasingly defined in code rather than configured through GUIs. Tools like Terraform, Bicep (Microsoft's native IaC language for Azure), ARM templates, and Ansible allow entire AVD environments to be defined as code, version-controlled, peer-reviewed, and deployed repeatably. This is Infrastructure as Code (IaC) — and it borrows heavily from DevOps culture.

For EUC teams, IaC means environments can be rebuilt identically in minutes, configuration drift is detectable, and "works on my machine" becomes "works in code." Nerdio's automation engine is built on top of Azure ARM and PowerShell at its core — IaC in practice.

DevOps Meets EUC

The cultural shift, not just the tooling

DevOps — the practice of breaking down walls between development and operations teams, automating everything, and shipping continuously — has fundamentally changed how modern EUC environments are managed. Image pipelines now use CI/CD principles: code a change → automated build → automated test → staged deployment. Golden image management in AVD increasingly mirrors how software developers ship code.

Tools: Azure DevOps, GitHub Actions, Packer (automated image builds), Bicep/Terraform, PowerShell DSC. The EUC professional of the future is increasingly a DevOps practitioner who happens to work on desktops.

Cloud Software Group · The Citrix Reckoning · 2022–Present

To understand what is happening to Citrix today, you need to understand what happened to VMware yesterday. The same private equity logic. The same software monetisation playbook. 🔁
The Broadcom Software Playbook — Understanding Hock Tan's Model
Acquire dominant software. Cut costs. Raise prices. Extract value.
To understand Cloud Software Group, you must first understand Broadcom CEO Hock Tan's acquisition philosophy. Broadcom — a semiconductor company — began acquiring enterprise software businesses not to build products, but to monetise installed bases. The logic is precise: identify software with high switching costs embedded in large enterprises, acquire it, dramatically cut R&D and headcount, end perpetual licensing, move customers to subscription, and raise prices to extract maximum value from a captive installed base. Tan applied this formula to Symantec (cybersecurity, 2019), CA Technologies (enterprise IT, 2018), and most famously VMware ($69B, 2023). In each case, the pattern was the same: partner programme disruption, headcount cuts (15–25%), perpetual licence elimination, subscription-only transition, and price increases that shocked the market. The VMware playbook — once executed — became the lens through which every Citrix customer would watch their own renewal cycle.
💰
January 2022 — Vista Equity Partners Acquires Citrix for $16.5B
Citrix goes private — and the monetisation clock starts
Vista Equity Partners and Elliott Investment Management acquired Citrix Systems for approximately $16.5 billion in January 2022, taking it private. This was one of the largest software leveraged buyouts in history. Vista simultaneously owned TIBCO Software (a data integration and analytics platform) and immediately announced the intent to merge it with Citrix into a new parent entity: Cloud Software Group (CSG). Private equity ownership fundamentally changes the strategic calculus: the goal shifts from long-term product investment to value extraction within a 5–7 year holding period. Customers who understood PE dynamics saw the writing on the wall immediately. Those who didn't were about to find out.
👤
July–September 2022 — Tom Krause Takes the Helm
The Broadcom alumnus who knows exactly how this plays out
Vista and Elliott's choice of CEO was the most significant signal of intent. Tom Krause was recruited directly from Broadcom, where he had served as CFO and then President of the Broadcom Software Group — the division that had executed the Symantec and CA acquisitions. Crucially, Krause was a key architect of the Broadcom strategy to acquire VMware. He did not just observe the PE software monetisation playbook from a distance — he helped write it. When Citrix customers heard that the new CEO of their vendor had spent six years at Broadcom building the exact model being applied to VMware, the alarm bells were clear. Krause took the CEO role in September 2022. Within months, Cloud Software Group laid off approximately 15% of the combined workforce. A year later, another 12% followed. The message to the market was unmistakable.
📈
2023 — The Pricing Changes Begin
8% across the board, end of perpetual, and mandatory subscription
The pricing actions came in waves. In March 2023, CSG announced that perpetual licence renewals for Citrix Virtual Apps and Desktops would be retired — organisations above 250 seats would need to move to subscription. In April 2023, an 8% price increase was applied across all Citrix products in all geographies, attributed to inflation and rising costs. NetScaler perpetual licensing ended. Customers were steered toward a minimum three-year upfront subscription commitment. The consolidation of product SKUs into bundled "Citrix Platform Licenses" (Universal and Platform tiers) — ostensibly to reduce complexity — in practice forced customers who needed specific components to buy everything in a bundle, paying for capabilities they didn't need. Rivals noted that the switch from perpetual to subscription had, in some cases, caused "staggering" price increases for customers.
📈
March–September 2024 — The Price Increases Escalate
Monthly subscriptions double. Channel partners face reduced rebates.
From September 1st, 2024, Citrix doubled the price of monthly subscription licences. Channel partner rebates were simultaneously reduced, leaving partners dependent on margin from services rather than software. When the changes were communicated to a gathering of Citrix partners, the reaction was described as "stunned silence followed by anger and disbelief." The parallel to the Broadcom-VMware transition was now explicit in the market. Partners and customers began evaluating alternatives in earnest. CSG's public response was to launch migration incentive programmes — discounts for customers moving from VMware or F5 onto the Citrix Platform — but these did little to address the core concern: that the pricing direction of travel had been set and would not reverse under PE ownership.
🚪
January 2025 — Tom Krause Departs for DOGE / US Treasury
A notable exit — and what it signals
In January 2025, Krause became involved with the US Treasury Department as part of the Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE), assigned as a special government employee. In February 2025 he was appointed as the Fiscal Assistant Secretary of the Treasury. His departure from day-to-day CSG leadership created leadership uncertainty at an already turbulent time for the business. Sridhar Mullapudi — a 25-year Citrix veteran — continues as Co-President and GM of the Citrix business unit. The departure did not change the underlying PE ownership dynamic or the pricing trajectory already in motion; the strategic direction was set. But it added another layer of uncertainty to a platform that customers were already questioning.

🚀 The Case for Migrating Away from Citrix

Why many customers are leaving — or planning to

  • Cost trajectory: Subscription prices are rising; the direction of travel under PE ownership is clear. A 3-year locked subscription at today's rates may look worse than today's rate at renewal time 3 years from now.
  • Bundle forcing: The "Universal" platform licence includes NetScaler, DaaS, and other components. Customers who only needed CVAD are now paying for capabilities they don't use.
  • The VMware warning: Organisations that watched what happened to VMware customers post-Broadcom are making strategic decisions not to be in the same position with Citrix in 3–5 years.
  • Microsoft's improving platform: AVD has matured significantly since 2019. The protocol (RDP Shortpath), graphics quality, Teams optimisation, and management tooling have all improved. The gap between Citrix HDX and AVD/RDP in most real-world use cases has narrowed substantially.
  • Nerdio as the enabler: The operational complexity argument — "AVD is too hard to manage without Citrix" — collapses when Nerdio is in the picture. Organisations moving to AVD no longer need to accept raw Azure complexity in place of Citrix complexity.
  • Licensing simplification: Microsoft 365 E3/E5 includes the entitlement to run AVD, FSLogix, and Windows 10/11 multi-session. For organisations already paying for M365, the marginal cost of AVD infrastructure is just Azure compute — which auto-scales with Nerdio.
⚠️ The Case for Staying on Citrix — For Now

Why migration is not simple — and many will wait

  • Migration complexity is real: Citrix environments built over 15–20 years are not switched off overnight. Application compatibility testing, profile migration (often thousands of users), network reconfiguration, ADC/load balancer replacement (NetScaler), and end-user retraining represent 12–24 month programmes, not projects.
  • Risk appetite: In regulated industries (FSI, healthcare, government), the risk of a failed desktop migration is not acceptable. Finance directors and CISOs who lived through a VDI failure in the 2000s are not eager to repeat the experience. "If it's working, the pain of change needs to exceed the pain of staying."
  • NetScaler dependency: Many large Citrix customers also run NetScaler (Citrix ADC) for application delivery and load balancing — a separate product from the virtual desktop stack. Replacing NetScaler is an independent project with its own complexity. This creates a "hostage" dynamic: even customers who want to migrate VDI are reluctant to trigger two simultaneous migrations.
  • ICA/HDX protocol advantages: For specific use cases — graphics-intensive workloads, very high-latency WAN connections, multimedia-rich environments — Citrix's HDX protocol still outperforms AVD's RDP Shortpath in measured performance. These edge cases matter to specific customer segments.
  • Renewal leverage: Customers in active renewal negotiations have found that CSG will discount — sometimes significantly — to retain large accounts. The published price list is not always the final price. Organisations with leverage (large seat counts, strategic relationships) can negotiate.
  • Skill and resource constraints: Many IT teams are running hot. A major platform migration requires dedicated resource, budget approval, and executive sponsorship. Even motivated teams may have a 12–18 month runway before migration can realistically begin.

Device Management · 1990s to Today

From Server Rooms to Every PocketHow managing endpoints went from physical visits to AI-driven zero-touch — and why it's now the core of EUC.

Endpoint management is arguably the most consequential thread running through all of EUC history. Every era introduced new device types that IT had to manage without breaking what came before. The story runs from manual server configuration through SMS, SCCM, BlackBerry, AirWatch, Good, and eventually converges on the Microsoft Intune + Entra ID platform that underpins every modern EUC deployment — including Nerdio's.

🖥️
Early 1990s · The Manual Era
Sneakernet and physical visits
Managing PCs meant walking to each desk with a floppy disk. Software distribution was physical. Patching was irregular. Inventory was a spreadsheet someone maintained manually and forgot to update. There were no remote management tools. If a PC had a virus, you went to it. IT headcount was directly proportional to device count — one admin per ~50 machines was considered efficient.
⚙️
1994 · Microsoft SMS — Systems Management Server
The first attempt at managing PCs at scale
Microsoft's Systems Management Server (SMS) was the first major enterprise platform for remote software distribution, inventory collection, and patch deployment across Windows PCs. It was complex, needed significant infrastructure of its own, required a dedicated SQL Server database, and was notoriously difficult to get right. But it established the concept: a central server should know about every endpoint and be able to push changes to them. SMS 1.0 required agents on every machine and communicated over the corporate LAN — you had to be on the network.
🔧
1996–2007 · Microsoft Operations Manager → SCOM
Monitoring the infrastructure, not just the endpoints
Microsoft Operations Manager (MOM), later renamed System Center Operations Manager (SCOM), addressed a parallel problem: how do you know if your servers, applications, and infrastructure are healthy? SCOM deployed agents across servers and used management packs (rules for specific applications like Exchange, SQL, IIS) to monitor performance, raise alerts, and track health state. In virtualised VDI environments, SCOM became essential for monitoring the hypervisor layer, session hosts, and connection brokers. IT operations teams lived in SCOM dashboards. It remains in use in many large enterprises today — though it's increasingly being displaced by Azure Monitor, Microsoft Sentinel, and cloud-native observability tools.
🏗️
2003 · SCCM — System Center Configuration Manager
SMS evolves into the dominant Windows management platform
SMS 2003 became SCCM (System Center Configuration Manager) — the enterprise Windows management standard for the next 15 years. SCCM handled software deployment, OS imaging and deployment (via PXE boot and task sequences), hardware and software inventory, patch management (via WSUS integration), compliance reporting, and remote control. At peak, SCCM managed tens of millions of Windows endpoints worldwide. It required on-premises infrastructure: a site server, SQL database, distribution points, and management points. Every large enterprise had a dedicated SCCM team. The product became so deeply embedded that organisations still run it today — often in "co-management" alongside Intune.
📟
1999–2013 · BlackBerry — The First Enterprise Mobile Management
The device that invented mobile security for business
BlackBerry (Research In Motion) didn't just make phones — it invented the concept of secure enterprise mobile communication. The BlackBerry Enterprise Server (BES) sat inside the corporate firewall, encrypted email end-to-end, and gave IT complete control over devices: remote wipe, PIN policies, application whitelisting, data separation. Banks, law firms, government departments, and regulated industries mandated BlackBerry. At peak (2009–2010), RIM had 85M subscribers and 50%+ of US smartphone market share. The problem: the iPhone arrived in 2007. BlackBerry dismissed it. By 2013, BlackBerry's enterprise dominance was essentially over — but the concepts it pioneered (remote wipe, device policy, enterprise email separation, containerisation) became the DNA of every MDM/EMM platform that followed.
📱
2007–2010 · iPhone, BYOD & the MDM Crisis
IT loses control of the device fleet overnight
The iPhone (June 2007) and Android (2008) detonated the enterprise device management model. Employees demanded to use their personal smartphones for work email. IT, used to owning every device, suddenly faced millions of unmanaged, heterogeneous, personally-owned devices touching corporate data. BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) policies emerged — a grudging acknowledgement that IT couldn't control what hardware people used. A new category was born: Mobile Device Management (MDM). Apple released its MDM protocol in iOS 4 (2010), enabling third parties to manage iPhones and iPads. The race was on.
📲
2007–2015 · AirWatch, MobileIron, Good Technology
The MDM wars — a fragmented market of specialist vendors
A wave of MDM/EMM (Enterprise Mobility Management) vendors emerged to manage the mobile chaos: AirWatch (founded 2003, MDM pivot 2007) became the market leader — comprehensive, policy-rich, enterprise-grade. MobileIron (2007) focused on security-first mobile management. Good Technology (originally Motorola Good, then Good Technology) pioneered containerisation — separating corporate apps and data from personal apps in a secure "Good container" on the same device. This was a critical innovation: it meant IT could wipe corporate data without wiping the employee's personal photos. Citrix XenMobile entered the space with deep app virtualisation integration. JAMF owned Apple device management in education and creative industries. Each vendor had proprietary consoles, proprietary agent technologies, and incompatible policy models. Managing a mixed iOS/Android/Windows fleet meant three different consoles.
🔀
2014–2018 · Consolidation — Microsoft Acquires, Microsoft Wins
The market collapses into the Microsoft ecosystem
VMware acquired AirWatch for $1.54B in February 2014 — the largest enterprise mobility acquisition at the time. IBM acquired MobileIron in 2020. Good Technology was acquired by BlackBerry in 2015. Microsoft, meanwhile, had been building Intune — initially a modest cloud MDM service — and began investing heavily. The key strategic decision: bundle Intune into Microsoft Enterprise Mobility + Security (EMS), then into Microsoft 365. Suddenly, every organisation with Microsoft 365 E3 or E5 had an MDM platform included. The specialist MDM vendors couldn't compete with "it's already in your M365 licence." AirWatch (now VMware Workspace ONE) survived as a credible enterprise alternative, but Intune became the default for Microsoft-centric organisations — which is most of the world.
☁️
2016–Present · Microsoft Intune & Modern Management
Cloud-native, policy-driven, agent-light endpoint management
Microsoft Intune (now part of Microsoft Endpoint Manager/Intune Suite) became the cloud-native answer to SCCM's on-premises complexity. Intune manages Windows, macOS, iOS, Android, and Linux from a single cloud portal — no on-premises infrastructure required. "Modern management" replaces image-based deployment with Autopilot (zero-touch provisioning: device arrives at employee's desk, they sign in with Entra ID, and it self-configures) and policy-driven configuration (no more group policy objects replicated from domain controllers). Co-management lets organisations run SCCM and Intune simultaneously during transition. The EUC world converged: Intune manages physical endpoints; AVD/Windows 365 manages virtual ones; both are enforced by Entra ID Conditional Access and the same security baselines. Nerdio's "Intune Insights" feature extends unified visibility across both virtual and physical devices — the management pane that sits above both.
🤖
2023–Present · AI-Driven Endpoint Management
From policy enforcement to predictive, autonomous management
The latest evolution moves from reactive management (detect problem → remediate) to predictive management (identify risk patterns → auto-remediate before impact). Microsoft Copilot in Intune can generate policy configurations from natural language. Endpoint Analytics predicts devices likely to have performance problems. Auto-remediation scripts fix common issues without tickets. Windows Autopatch automates patch deployment and rollback. The management platform is becoming a self-managing system — with the human in the loop for exception handling, not routine operations. For AVD environments managed by Nerdio, this extends to session host health: auto-heal restarts broken VMs, auto-scaling adjusts capacity based on usage signals, and image pipelines deploy updates on schedules without manual intervention.

Hyperscalers & Hardware · The Platform Wars

Who Built the Infrastructure EUC Runs OnCloud giants, hardware makers, and thin clients — and why Microsoft won the EUC cloud war.

EUC doesn't exist in isolation. It runs on infrastructure — cloud platforms, physical hardware, networking equipment, and endpoint devices. The hyperscaler race between Microsoft, AWS, and Google fundamentally shaped which cloud desktop platform won. And the hardware ecosystem — Dell, HP, Lenovo, Apple, IGEL, Google — continues to evolve around the shift from thick clients to thin clients to cloud-managed devices.

The Hyperscaler Race

Cloud Giants & the EUC Platform War

🪟 Microsoft Azure — The Winner

Why Microsoft won EUC's cloud war

Microsoft's path to EUC dominance was neither obvious nor inevitable. Azure launched in 2010 — years behind AWS. But Microsoft had an asymmetric advantage: it owned Windows, Office, and Active Directory — the three things enterprises couldn't easily move away from. When Nadella pivoted in 2014 to "cloud-first," the strategic logic was simple: move the stuff organisations already depend on (Office → Office 365, AD → Entra ID, Windows → AVD/W365) to Azure, and they'll follow.

Azure Virtual Desktop, launching September 2019, was the culmination: a cloud-native virtual desktop infrastructure that only Microsoft could offer — Windows 10/11 multi-session licensing (a SKU available nowhere else), native Entra ID integration, FSLogix included free, and deep parity with every Microsoft 365 service. No other hyperscaler could replicate this. AWS and Google sell IaaS; Microsoft sells a complete, integrated EUC stack. That's why it won.

📦 AWS — The IaaS Giant That Missed EUC

The infrastructure giant without a Windows advantage

Amazon Web Services launched in 2006 and dominated cloud infrastructure for a decade. AWS had WorkSpaces (cloud desktop service, launched 2013) — a direct AVD competitor. But WorkSpaces never achieved meaningful market share in Windows desktop delivery. Why? AWS doesn't own Windows, Office, or Active Directory. Every WorkSpaces deployment required Windows Server licensing from Microsoft. Microsoft's multi-session Windows 10/11 — available only in Azure — made AVD dramatically cheaper for organisations running shared desktops. AWS CompSpaces are most common in Linux developer environments and niche use cases, not mainstream enterprise EUC.

AWS's EUC contribution: AppStream 2.0 (application streaming, not full desktop) found traction in education and kiosk scenarios. But AWS's strength is its breadth — 200+ cloud services — not specifically EUC. Most enterprises use AWS alongside Azure for workloads, with Azure holding the EUC desktop.

🔵 Google Cloud — The Chromebook Play

Owning the endpoint, not the desktop

Google's EUC strategy differed fundamentally from both Microsoft and AWS. Rather than competing on virtual Windows desktop delivery, Google attacked the endpoint — Chrome OS and Chromebook devices. In education, Google won decisively: affordable, easy to manage, zero local software, everything browser-based. In K-12 education globally, Chromebooks now hold over 60% market share.

Google Workspace (formerly G Suite) and Chrome Enterprise are Google's EUC play: managed ChromeOS devices, browser-based productivity apps, and Google Cloud as the backend. For Windows-centric organisations, Google Cloud Desktop-as-a-Service delivers Windows through a browser — but with the same fundamental limitation as AWS: no access to Microsoft's multi-session Windows licensing. Google's strongest EUC position is as an endpoint (Chromebooks as AVD thin clients) rather than a desktop platform — a use case that pairs well with Nerdio-managed AVD environments.

🟢 VMware — The On-Premises EUC Platform That Lost the Cloud War

Dominant on-prem, outmanoeuvred in cloud

VMware was the dominant EUC infrastructure platform for over a decade. VMware vSphere powered the hypervisor. VMware Horizon delivered VDI. VMware NSX handled the network. VMware Workspace ONE managed devices. It was a complete, if complex, on-premises EUC stack. The problem: when the cloud transition accelerated, VMware had no Azure equivalent. Horizon could run on Azure — but customers were just running VMware's software on Microsoft's infrastructure. Broadcom's acquisition in 2023 then destroyed the goodwill, pricing trust, and partner relationships VMware had spent 25 years building.

Omnissa, the spun-off Horizon/Workspace ONE entity, continues — but carries the brand damage and pricing uncertainty that Broadcom created. The Broadcom-VMware implosion accelerated Microsoft's AVD dominance more than any product launch could have.

Apple

The premium endpoint that IT finally had to support

Apple's entry into enterprise EUC was employee-driven rather than IT-driven. Creative professionals, executives, and software developers demanded Macs. IT departments had no tools to manage them — until JAMF emerged as the Apple Enterprise Management platform of choice. Today, Apple has a dedicated enterprise programme, Device Enrolment Program (DEP/Apple Business Manager), and works with Microsoft so that Macs can be enrolled in Intune alongside Windows devices. iPhones and iPads are managed via Intune MDM profiles. In EUC, Apple devices typically access Windows applications via AVD in a browser or the Windows App (formerly Remote Desktop app) — making them valid AVD thin clients. Increasingly, organisations run a mixed estate: Macs for power users, Windows 365 Cloud PCs for their Windows application access.

🖥️
IGEL Technology

The thin client software play that reimagined the endpoint

IGEL took a different approach to thin clients: instead of selling hardware, sell a Linux-based OS that converts any x86 device into a managed thin client. IGEL OS transforms a retired Windows PC, a Dell Wyse, or any compatible hardware into a lightweight, centrally-managed endpoint that connects to cloud desktops (AVD, Citrix, VMware, AWS WorkSpaces) via secure remote protocols. The IGEL Universal Management Suite (UMS) provides centralised policy management for every IGEL endpoint. In regulated industries where endpoint security is paramount — finance, healthcare, government — IGEL's read-only OS (nothing writes to local storage, all data lives in the cloud desktop) is a compelling security posture. IGEL integrates directly with Nerdio-managed AVD environments and is a frequent joint solution in enterprise deployments.

🔵
Google ChromeOS / Chromebook

The thin client that won education and is entering enterprise

Google's ChromeOS has succeeded as an enterprise thin client where dedicated thin client hardware struggled. Chromebooks are inexpensive, zero-maintenance (updates are automatic, OS is read-only, devices reset in 8 seconds), and centrally managed via Google Admin Console. In education, they dominate. In healthcare, ChromeOS-based thin clients connect to clinical applications via Citrix or AVD. In retail, Chrome Flex (ChromeOS on any x86 device) converts existing PCs into managed thin clients. Critically, Chromebooks work as AVD clients — accessing Microsoft AVD sessions through the Windows App browser experience — making them viable endpoints in Nerdio-managed environments. The endpoint cost drops dramatically; the management moves to the cloud desktop.

Every Industry Has Its Own EUC StoryWhy regulated, distributed, and data-intensive sectors drove EUC adoption — and where they're headed.

EUC didn't evolve uniformly across industries. Regulated sectors adopted thin-client and VDI earliest because data residency and security drove the architecture. Education discovered cloud desktops through budget constraints. Retail needed distributed management at massive scale. Manufacturing grappled with OT/IT convergence. Each vertical shaped EUC's history — and each has a distinct path to modern cloud desktop architecture.

Financial Services — Banking, Investment, Wealth Management

From trading floor mainframes to cloud desktops under FCA scrutiny

Then

Financial services were among the earliest enterprise VDI adopters — driven by regulatory requirements (FCA, PRA, MiFID II, Basel III) that mandated data residency, audit trails, and the ability to prove what a trader had access to and when. Trading floors ran Bloomberg terminals, Reuters feeds, and proprietary execution systems on dedicated hardware. The idea of data leaving the building was anathema. Citrix was ubiquitous — centralising sensitive financial data in the datacentre while delivering it to trading desks, branch offices, and remote workers was exactly what Citrix was built for.

Now

COVID forced rapid remote-working adoption in a sector that had resisted it. Banks and asset managers deployed AVD at pace. DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act) and FCA operational resilience requirements now mandate business continuity that cloud desktops inherently provide — if the office floods, employees access the same AVD session from home. The challenge: sovereignty and data residency. EU financial institutions must prove data stays in specific geographic regions. Azure's EU Data Boundary and sovereign cloud capabilities address this. Zero Trust architecture (Conditional Access, MFA, device compliance) is now a regulatory expectation, not a nice-to-have.

AI Copilot in financial workflows creates new EUC governance requirements: which users can access Copilot, what data can it see, how is it audited? The secure, governed AVD environment — managed by Nerdio with role-based access controls, approval workflows, and audit logging — becomes the secure container for AI tool deployment. FSI firms like Schroders, MUFG, and SMBC are evaluating AVD/Nerdio precisely because of this governance story alongside cost optimisation versus legacy Citrix estates.

Insurance — Life, General, Lloyd's, Reinsurance

Distributed workforces, legacy actuarial systems, and Solvency II

Then

Insurance companies ran notoriously complex IT estates — proprietary policy administration systems built in the 1980s and 90s, running on AS/400 or mainframe systems, wrapped in Citrix or Terminal Services to give claims handlers and underwriters access. The Lloyd's market in London was particularly complex: syndicates and managing agents sharing Lloyd's infrastructure but each maintaining separate IT environments. Branch office networks were expensive MPLS circuits. Remote access for field agents was a constant challenge.

Now

Solvency II (EU) and equivalent capital adequacy frameworks require insurers to prove operational resilience and data governance. The hybrid workforce model — underwriters in London, claims handlers distributed, actuaries anywhere — has driven AVD adoption as the consistent delivery mechanism for both modern SaaS tools and legacy insurance platforms. Direct Line, Aviva, AXA, and major Lloyd's syndicates are all evaluating or deploying cloud desktops as part of Citrix migration programmes accelerated by Cloud Software Group pricing changes.

Healthcare — NHS, Hospital Groups, Life Sciences, Pharma

Roaming clinicians, patient data, GDPR and the WannaCry wake-up call

Then

Healthcare was a late and reluctant VDI adopter. Clinical applications (EPR systems like Epic, Cerner, System C) were complex, performance-sensitive, and often required local graphics acceleration. The "roaming clinician" problem — a nurse or doctor who moves between wards, departments, and sites and needs their session to follow them instantly — was a key VDI use case. Citrix delivered this. The WannaCry ransomware attack of May 2017, which devastated NHS England (47 trusts affected, 19,000 cancelled appointments), was the most consequential cybersecurity event in healthcare IT history — and a brutal demonstration of what happens when endpoints aren't patched and managed.

Now

Post-WannaCry, NHS and private healthcare accelerated endpoint modernisation. GDPR (2018) and the Data Security and Protection Toolkit (DSPT) in the UK mandate demonstrable data governance for all NHS suppliers and trusts. Cloud desktops — where clinical data never touches the endpoint — are an architecturally strong GDPR and DSPT response. Windows 365 Cloud PCs are increasingly deployed for admin and back-office NHS staff. Epic and Cerner have both validated AVD as a supported delivery mechanism. IGEL thin clients are common in hospital wards — low-cost, zero-local-data, easy to wipe and reuse.

Tomorrow + Nerdio

AI diagnostics and clinical decision support tools require compliant, auditable, low-latency compute. The AVD session becomes the secure clinical workspace: patient data stays in Azure, AI tools operate within the governed environment, every interaction is auditable. Nerdio's security posture — deployed within the customer's own Azure subscription, CIS Benchmark Intune baselines, Conditional Access integration — directly maps to NHS DSPT requirements and ICO guidance.

Education — K-12, Higher Education, Universities

From computer labs to cloud classrooms — and every student's own device

Then

Education was defined by the computer lab — a room of shared PCs managed by an overworked IT technician. Universities ran shared VDI pools for specialist software (AutoCAD, MATLAB, SPSS, Adobe Creative Suite) because students couldn't afford the software or the hardware to run it. The lab booking system was the bottleneck. Microsoft's Imagine (formerly DreamSpark) and educational volume licensing made Windows and Office affordable, but software specialisation requirements remained complex.

Google won K-12 with Chromebooks — cheap, manageable, browser-centric. Microsoft is winning it back through Windows 365 Education and Azure Lab Services. Universities are deploying AVD to replace physical computer labs: instead of buying 200 CAD workstations, deploy 200 GPU-enabled AVD sessions accessible from any device. Students access specialist software from their laptop, tablet, or home PC. COVID forced this: when universities closed in March 2020, institutions with cloud-based labs maintained continuity; those with physical labs cancelled practicals for a year.

Manufacturing & Industrial — Automotive, Aerospace, Heavy Industry

OT meets IT — the factory floor finally joins the managed estate

Then

Manufacturing had two completely separate IT worlds: the corporate IT network (PCs, email, ERP) and the operational technology (OT) network (industrial control systems, PLCs, SCADA, CNC machines). These were air-gapped by design — the OT network never touched the internet. Designers ran CAD workstations (often expensive dedicated graphics hardware) in engineering. Factory floor workers had minimal PC access. Managing hundreds of geographically distributed plants with different regional IT teams was a patchwork of inconsistent configurations and unpatched systems.

Industry 4.0 — the convergence of physical and digital manufacturing — is breaking down the OT/IT divide. IoT sensors on machines send data to Azure. Digital twins model factory processes. Remote monitoring by central engineering teams requires engineers to access plant systems and design tools from any location. GPU-enabled AVD sessions deliver CAD applications (Siemens NX, PTC Creo, Autodesk) to engineers without dedicated workstations. Concepts & Terminology

The Terms Everyone Uses But Not Everyone Explains

VDI — Virtual Desktop Infrastructure

Running a user's Windows desktop as a virtual machine on a server in a datacentre, rather than on the user's physical PC. The user connects via a remote display protocol (RDP, ICA, Blast). Compute, storage, and OS all live centrally. The endpoint just renders the screen and sends input.

Traditional VDI = your infrastructure, your datacentre. Cloud VDI (AVD) = Microsoft's infrastructure, your configuration. Same concept, radically different operational model.

AVD — Azure Virtual Desktop

Microsoft's cloud-native virtual desktop platform, running entirely on Azure. Microsoft manages the infrastructure (host pools, session brokering, authentication gateway). You manage the virtual machines (session hosts), images, and configuration. Launched 2019. Includes Windows 10/11 multi-session — a unique licence available only in Azure that lets multiple users share one Windows client OS VM, dramatically reducing cost.

Windows 365 — Cloud PC

A simpler, fixed-price alternative to AVD. You pick a size (e.g. 2 vCPU / 8GB RAM / 128GB storage), assign it to a user, and they get a persistent Cloud PC — always theirs, always the same, accessible from any browser. Fixed monthly pricing per user. No Azure infrastructure expertise required. Less flexible than AVD but far simpler to operate. Best for organisations that want cloud desktops without complexity.

Zero Trust — Never Trust, Always Verify

A security philosophy (coined by John Kindervag, 2010) that rejects the perimeter security model. Instead of assuming everything inside the network is safe, Zero Trust requires every access request to be verified regardless of origin — checking: who is the user (identity), what device are they on (compliance), where are they (location/risk), and what are they trying to access (application sensitivity).

In EUC practice, Zero Trust is implemented through Microsoft Entra ID (identity), Intune (device compliance), Conditional Access (policy engine), and Microsoft Defender (threat signals). Every AVD login, every SaaS app access, every API call is evaluated against these signals before access is granted.

Conditional Access

Microsoft Entra ID's policy engine for implementing Zero Trust. Evaluates signals (identity + device compliance + location + app sensitivity + risk score) and makes an access decision: allow, block, or require additional verification (MFA, device enrolment, Terms of Use acceptance). Conditional Access policies control what users can access AVD and Windows 365 environments, from which devices, and under what conditions. Understanding CA is essential for any EUC architect deploying on Microsoft's cloud platform.

DEX — Digital Employee Experience

The measurement and optimisation of how effectively technology serves employees. Goes beyond infrastructure health ("the server is up") to measure what users actually experience: how fast do apps launch, how reliable is the network, how long does login take, how often do applications crash. DEX platforms (Lakeside, ControlUp, Nexthink) instrument thousands of data points per session to drive both reactive support (diagnose why user X is having a bad day) and proactive optimisation (spot patterns before users complain). Post-COVID, DEX became a boardroom metric — the equivalent of a customer satisfaction score, but for internal technology.

SD-WAN — Software-Defined Wide Area Networking

A networking architecture that separates the network control plane from the physical hardware, enabling intelligent traffic routing across multiple connection types (MPLS, broadband, LTE, 5G). SD-WAN can direct Office 365 traffic directly to the internet (local breakout) while sending sensitive corporate traffic through a central security hub — optimising both performance and cost. Critical for EUC because cloud desktop performance depends heavily on network quality and routing decisions. A poorly routed AVD session will feel terrible even if the Azure infrastructure is perfect.

SASE — Secure Access Service Edge

A framework (Gartner, 2019) that converges network and security services into a single cloud-delivered platform. Combines SD-WAN, ZTNA (Zero Trust Network Access), CASB (Cloud Access Security Broker), Secure Web Gateway, and Firewall-as-a-Service. Instead of managing separate products for each capability, SASE vendors (Zscaler, Palo Alto Prisma Access, Cloudflare) deliver a unified service that inspects and routes traffic securely regardless of user location. For EUC, SASE defines how users (in an office, at home, in an AVD session) securely reach applications wherever they live.

Infrastructure as Code (IaC)

Managing and provisioning infrastructure through machine-readable definition files (code) rather than manual processes or GUI clicks. Tools: Terraform (multi-cloud, declarative), Bicep (Microsoft's native Azure IaC language), ARM Templates, Ansible (configuration management). In EUC, IaC means entire AVD environments — host pools, session hosts, networking, policies, image pipelines — can be defined in code, version-controlled in Git, reviewed, tested, and deployed repeatably. Reduces configuration drift (when the live environment no longer matches the intended configuration) and enables disaster recovery at speed.

FSLogix — Profile Containers

A profile management technology that mounts user profiles as VHD(X) disk containers rather than copying files across the network at login. This makes profile loading near-instant, eliminates corruption, and works correctly with OneDrive, Teams, Outlook search indexing, and other Microsoft applications that break in traditional roaming profile scenarios. Microsoft acquired FSLogix in 2018 and made it free — it now ships with every Microsoft 365 subscription. FSLogix Profile Container is a mandatory component of virtually every AVD deployment.

SaaS — Software as a Service

Software delivered via the internet on a subscription basis, hosted and operated by the vendor. The user accesses it through a browser — no local installation, no local updates, no local server required. Examples: Microsoft 365 (email, Office apps, Teams), Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow, Slack, Zoom. SaaS adoption accelerated the rationale for cloud desktops: if your applications are already in the cloud, the argument for running Windows in the cloud (rather than managing physical PCs) becomes much stronger. SaaS also changed the network model — traffic goes to the internet, not to the datacentre, which is why SD-WAN and SASE matter.

DevOps in EUC Context

DevOps is a culture and set of practices that combines software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops) with the goal of shortening development cycles and delivering changes continuously. In EUC, DevOps principles now apply to: golden image pipelines (automated builds with Packer, tested with automated tooling, deployed through staged rings), policy management (Intune configurations in Git, reviewed and deployed via CI/CD), and environment provisioning (Terraform/Bicep scripts version-controlled and deployed automatically). The EUC professional who understands Git, pipelines, and IaC is increasingly more valuable than one who only knows how to click through GUIs.
